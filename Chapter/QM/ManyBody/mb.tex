In framework of many-body system, the fundamental object is the many-body wavefunction
\[
  \Psi(x_1,\ldots,x_N)
  =\Psi(\mathbf{r}_1,\sigma_1\dots,\mathbf{r}_N,\sigma_N)
\]
where the generalized coordinate $x = (\mathbf{r}, \sigma)$ takes into account the spatial coordinate $\mathbf{r}$ but also the intrinsic spin $\sigma$ pertaining to the particle.
The state of the system therefore lives in the tensor product Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes N}$.
For identical particles, the wavefunction must satisfy permutation symmetry,
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi(\ldots, x_i, \ldots, x_j, \ldots) = \pm \Psi(\ldots, x_j, \ldots, x_i, \ldots)
\end{equation*}

When the particle number becomes macroscopic $  N \rightarrow O(10^{23})$ and the system is described only through macroscopic observable rather than the full wavefunction, one replaces the pure state description $\Psi(x_1,\ldots,x_N)$ with an ensemble or density operator
\begin{equation*}
  \rho = \frac{e^{-\beta H}}{Z}, \quad Z = \text{Tr} \, e^{-\beta H}.
\end{equation*}
This quantum statistical mechanics step corresponds to assuming statistical equilibrium with a reservoir (canonical ensemble) or fixed energy (microcanonical ensemble).

Thermodynamics emerges when an additional limit is taken
\begin{equation*}
  N \to \infty, \quad V \to \infty, \quad \frac{N}{V} = \text{const.}
\end{equation*}
In this regime microscopic fluctuations become negligible compared with macroscopic averages.
Observables become smooth functions of intensive variables such as \(T, P,\) and \(\rho\).
Thermodynamic relations follow from derivatives of the free energy
\begin{equation*}
  F = -k_B T \ln Z, \quad U = -\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \ln Z
\end{equation*}
For example
\begin{equation*}
  S = -\left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial T} \right)_V, \quad P = -\left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial V} \right)_T
\end{equation*}
Thermodynamic quantities are obtained from the partition function,

Solid-state theory arises when the many-body system satisfies essential conditions
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The particles form a dense phase (typically electrons and ions).
  \item The ions form an approximately periodic lattice
        \begin{equation*}
          V(\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{R}) = V(\mathbf{r})
        \end{equation*}
        where \(\mathbf{R}\) is a Bravais lattice vector. In this condition, eigenstates satisfy Bloch's theorem $\psi_{nk}(\mathbf{r}) = e^{i \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r}} u_{nk}(\mathbf{r})$
  \item The electronic states can be treated in an effective single-particle framework due to screening and mean-field approximations.
\end{enumerate}

The many-body problem is then approximated by quasiparticles (electrons, phonons, magnons) moving in effective potentials.
Techniques such as Hartree–Fock, density functional theory, or Fermi liquid theory implement this reduction.

\subsection{Identical Particle}
Before imposing indistinguishability, an $N$-particle state is written as
\begin{equation*}
  |k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_N\rangle = |k_1\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes |k_N\rangle
\end{equation*}
This state is not symmetric in general.
If we exchange two slots,
\begin{equation*}
  \mathcal{T}_{12}|k_1, k_2, \ldots \rangle = |k_2, k_1, \ldots \rangle
\end{equation*}
we obtain is a different vector.

Indistinguishability implies that the Hamiltonian cannot change if the degrees of freedom of two arbitrary particles $i$ and $j$ are exchanged
\begin{equation*}
  {H } = {H} (\dots,i\dots,j,\dots) = {H} (\dots,j\dots,i,\dots)
\end{equation*}
This can be seen by acting with the operator exchanging two arbitrary particles, $i$ and $j$, on a state of $N$ identical particles
\begin{equation*}
  \mathcal{T }_{ij} \ket{\dots, \phi_i,\dots,\phi_j,\dots} = \ket{\dots, \phi_j,\dots,\phi_i,\dots}
\end{equation*}
which produces a state that may not be physically different from the original state, so that there can only be a phase factor $\nu_{ij }=e ^{i\alpha_{ij}}$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathcal{T }_{ij} \ket{\dots, \phi_i,\dots,\phi_j,\dots} = \nu_{ij }\ket{\dots, \phi_i,\dots,\phi_j,\dots}
\end{equation*}
Moreover, because of $\mathcal{T}_{ij }=\mathcal{T}_{ji}$ we have
\begin{equation*}
  \mathcal{T}_{ij }^{2 }=1\qquad\nu_{ij }=\pm1
\end{equation*}
These results imply that there are only two types of quantum particles
\begin{equation*}
  \nu = \begin{cases}
    +1, & \text{ particles whose symmetrical under particle exchange}     \\
    -1, & \text{ particles whose antisymmetrical under particle exchange}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection{Boson.}
Consider the state
\begin{equation*}
  |k_1, \ldots, k_N\rangle = |k_1\rangle \otimes |k_2\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes |k_N\rangle
\end{equation*}
The subscript in $\ket{k_n}$ labels the one-particle state in that tensor slot.
In order to produce physical multiparticle boson state, where the particles are truly indistinguishable, we introduce symmetrization $S$ for a fixed number $N$ of particles
\begin{equation*}
  S |k_1, \ldots, k_N \rangle =
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!N_1! \ldots N_M!}}
  \sum_{p \in S_N} |k_{p(1)}, \ldots, k_{p(N)} \rangle
\end{equation*}
which satisfy $\nu=1$.
The symmetrization operator  $S$ acts on the tensor-product slots, not on the physical particles themselves, where it rearranges the positions of the one-particle states in the tensor product
\begin{equation*}
  |k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_N\rangle \rightarrow |k_{p(1)}, k_{p(2)}, \ldots, k_{p(N)}\rangle
\end{equation*}

The is run over $p$ all permutations $p$ of the set $\mathcal{N}=\left\{ 1,\dots,N \right\} $, which there are $N!$ element, hence the $1/\sqrt{N!}$ normalization constant.

Now suppose that $N_l$ particle occupy $k_l$ state.
Many permutations now produce identical terms, since swapping two particles that are both in $k_l$  does not change the vector, hence $1/\sqrt{N_l!}$ normalization constant.

Each term is the original tensor-product state with tensor slots rearranged according to $p$, such that $\ket{\dots k_{p(n)}\dots}$ is placed in slot $n$.
For example, with $p(n)=n+1$, the state $S\ket{k_1}$ gets mapped into
\begin{equation*}
  \ket{k_{{(1)}}\dots} \mapsto \ket{\dots \ket{k_2}\dots}
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection{Fermion.}
With the same state $|k_1, \ldots, k_N\rangle $, the antisymmetrization is defined as
\begin{equation*}
  A|k_1,k_2,\ldots,k_N\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \sum_{p \in S_N} \mathrm{sgn}(P) |k_{i_1},k_{i_2},\ldots,k_{i_N}\rangle
\end{equation*}
As the case with the symmetrization operator, the sum is run over $p$ all permutations $p$ of the set $\left\{ 1,\dots,N \right\} $.
The only difference is in the parity factor
\begin{equation*}
  \mathrm{sgn}(p) =
  \begin{cases}
    +1 & \text{ Even permutations} \\
    -1 & \text{ Odd permutations}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation*}
This factor express that the state antisymmetry under particle exchange.
Since any permutation $p$ of $N$ slots can be written as a product of transpositions
\begin{equation*}
  p = \mathcal{T}_{i_1j_1} \mathcal{T}_{i_2j_2} \ldots\mathcal{T}_{i_mj_m}.
\end{equation*}
the parity of the permutation is determined by the number of transpositions $m$ in this decomposition: even $m$ for $+1$ and odd $m$ for $-1$.

This parity is the origin of Pauli exclusion principle.
The probability for a many-particle state to contain two or more Fermions in the same single-particle state vanishes.
In another important contribution, Wolfgang Pauli (1940) proved by his famous spin-statistics theorem that Bosons carry integer spin, $s=1,\dots$ , while Fermions carry half-integer spin, $s = 1/2, \dots$.

|\subsubsection{The application.}
The symmetric and antisymmetrical operator is used to construct boson and fermion subspace from the $n $-particle Hilbert space
\begin{equation*}
  \mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}  =
  \underbrace{\mathcal{H} \otimes \dots \otimes \mathcal{H}}_n =
  \bigotimes_n \mathcal{H}
\end{equation*}
The symmetric subspace is defined as the set of all vectors invariant under permutation of slots
\begin{equation*}
  \mathrm{Sym}^n(\mathcal{H}) = \left\{ |\Psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}^{\otimes n} \mid P|\Psi\rangle = |\Psi\rangle \text{ for all permutations } P \right\}
\end{equation*}
with $P_p$ as the linear operator representing $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}$.
Equivalently,
\begin{equation*}
  \mathrm{Sym}^n(\mathcal{H}) = S\mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}
\end{equation*}
where the symmetrization projector action is
\begin{equation*}
  S = \frac{1}{n!} \sum_{p \in S_n} P_p
\end{equation*}
Note that the normalization constant is $1/n!$ instead of $1/\sqrt{n!}$ to satisfy idempotency $S^2=S$ for subspace projection.
Meanwhile, the antisymmetrical subspace is defined as the set of vectors that change sign under odd permutations
\begin{equation*}
  \wedge^n(H) = \left\{ |\Psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}^{\otimes n} \,|\, P |\Psi\rangle = \mathrm{sgn}(P) |\Psi\rangle \right\}
\end{equation*}
Equivalently,
\begin{equation*}
  \wedge^n(H) = A \mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}
\end{equation*}
with the action of antisymmetrization as
\begin{equation*}
  A = \frac{1}{n!} \sum_{p \in S_n} \text{sgn}(p) P
\end{equation*}

With these in mind, the Fock subsspace for both boson and fermion can be derived from the full Fock space as
\begin{equation*}
  \mathcal{F}_B = \bigoplus_{n=0}^{\infty} \mathrm{Sym}^n(\mathcal{H}) \qquad
  \mathcal{F}_F = \bigoplus_{n=0}^{\infty} \wedge^n(\mathcal{H})
\end{equation*}

\subsection{Identical Particle Wavefunction}
The arbitrary product state $  |k_1, \ldots, k_N\rangle$, which do not in general obey symmetrization nor antisymmetrization condition, can be projected into boson or fermion subspace using symmetrization and antisymmetrization operator
\begin{gather*}
  S |k_1, \ldots, k_N \rangle =
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!N_1! N_1! \ldots N_M!}}
  \sum_{p \in S_N} |k_{p(1)}, \ldots, k_{p(N)} \rangle  \\
  A|k_1,k_2,\ldots,k_N\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \sum_{p \in S_N} \mathrm{sgn}(P) |k_{i_1},k_{i_2},\ldots,k_{i_N}\rangle
\end{gather*}
To determine the wavefunction of boson and fermion, one construct $N \times N$ matrix
\begin{equation*}
  M =
  \begin{bmatrix}
    \phi_1(x_1) & \ldots & \phi_1(x_N) \\
    \vdots      & \ddots & \vdots      \\
    \phi_N(x_1) & \ldots & \phi_N(x_N)
  \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
where $\phi_i $ denote quantum state and $\phi(x_j)$ labels the particle coordinate.

\subsubsection{Boson.}
The wavefunction  in boson subspace is represented as
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi_B(x_1, \ldots, x_N) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!N_1! \ldots N_M!}} \;\mathrm{perm}(M)
\end{equation*}
Written explicitly,
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi_B(x_1, \ldots, x_N)
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!\prod_i N_i!}} \sum_{P \in S_N} \prod_{i=1}^N \phi_i(x_{P(i)})
\end{equation*}

In the Bose–Einstein condensate, all $N$ particles occupy the same single–particle ground orbital $\phi_0(x)$, hence permutation produces the same term
\begin{equation*}
  \prod_{j=1}^{N} \phi_0(x_{P(j)}) = \phi_0(x_1)\phi_0(x_2)\cdots\phi_0(x_N)
\end{equation*}
Since there are $N!$ permutations
\begin{equation*}
  \sum_{P\in S_N} \prod_{j=1}^{N} \phi_0(x_{P(j)}) = N! \phi_0(x_1)\phi_0(x_2)\cdots\phi_0(x_N)
\end{equation*}
The normalization factor becomes
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!N!}} = \frac{1}{N!}
\end{equation*}
Hence the many-body wavefunction reduces to
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi(x_1,\ldots,x_N) = \phi_0(x_1)\phi_0(x_2)\cdots\phi_0(x_N)
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection{Fermion.}
Rather with permanent, we use determinant, also called Slater determinant
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi_F(x_1, \ldots, x_N) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \det(M)
\end{equation*}
Expanding the determinant gives
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi_F(x_1, \ldots, x_N) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \sum_{P \in S_N} \text{sgn}(P) \prod_{i=1}^N \phi_i(x_{P(i)})
\end{equation*}
The determinant will enforce sign change under particle exchange and vanishing upon particle occupying the same state.
